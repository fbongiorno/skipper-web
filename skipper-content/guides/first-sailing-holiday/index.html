Your First Sailing Holiday: The Complete First-Timer's Checklist

Nobody's first sailing holiday is perfect — but it can be close. This checklist covers everything first-timers wish they'd known before they cast off: the surprises, the essentials, and the things that make the difference between a good trip and a great one.

Before you book

The most important decision you'll make is not which destination to choose — it's what type of charter to book. If you have no sailing experience, you have three options: a flotilla charter (your own boat with a lead boat and professional support — the best first-timer option), a crewed charter (a yacht with a professional skipper, maximum luxury and zero sailing required), or taking a sailing course first and then booking a bareboat. Don't book a bareboat charter without sailing experience — it's genuinely dangerous and most charter companies won't allow it.

Choosing the right destination

For your first sailing holiday, the destination matters less than the sailing conditions. You want: short passages (under 3 hours), sheltered anchorages, reliable but moderate winds, and warm water. The BVI is the world's best first-timer destination for exactly these reasons. In Europe, the Ionian Islands (Greece) and the Dalmatian Coast (Croatia) are the best first choices. Avoid the Cyclades (meltemi winds too strong for beginners), Brittany (Atlantic tides too complex), and the Tuamotus (too remote and technical).

Flotilla vs. bareboat vs. crewed

Flotilla is the best choice for most first-timers. You have your own boat — the independence and adventure are real — but a lead boat with a professional skipper and hostess sails the same route and is available by VHF whenever you need help. You're never truly alone and never truly dependent. Flotilla holidays cost slightly more than a straight bareboat but significantly less than a fully crewed yacht.

Crewed charter is the right choice if you're not interested in sailing at all and want a luxury holiday on the water. A professional captain handles everything; you swim, sunbathe, and eat. More expensive than flotilla; completely stress-free.

Bareboat requires a qualification. If you want to skipper your own boat, take an RYA Day Skipper or ASA 104 course first.

What to expect on board

Charter yachts are smaller than you imagine. A 40ft yacht — which sounds large — has cabins about the size of a double bed with limited headroom. The heads (bathroom) are compact. The galley (kitchen) has two or three burners and a small oven. Storage is extremely limited — this is the most common first-timer surprise. Everything must be stowed before going to sea; nothing can be left loose on surfaces.

The boat moves constantly when underway. It also moves at anchor — a gentle roll in a swell or a swing on the anchor as the wind shifts. Some people find this impossible to sleep through on the first night; most adapt within 24 hours.

The first day

The handover day is always chaotic. The charter company briefing typically takes 2–3 hours; by the time you've received the boat, done the inventory, and provisioned, it's often mid-afternoon. Don't plan to sail far on day one — spend the first night in the marina or a nearby bay while you find where everything is, practice the anchor drill, and cook your first meal on board. The second day, properly rested and familiar with the boat, is when the holiday really begins.

Managing seasickness

Seasickness is more common on the first day than at any other point — the body hasn't adapted to the motion. The best prophylactic: take medication before you feel sick, not after. Scopolamine skin patches (prescription) applied 4 hours before departure are the most effective. Stugeron (cinnarizine) is available over-the-counter in Europe and the Caribbean. On board, stay on deck in fresh air, look at the horizon, avoid going below in rough conditions, and eat lightly. The second day is almost always better.

What to pack

Pack less than you think you need. One 60-litre soft bag per person maximum. Essential items: non-marking deck shoes (mandatory — don't arrive without them), a lightweight waterproof jacket, SPF 50+ sunscreen in large quantities, seasickness medication, polarised sunglasses, a swimsuit for every day, and smart-casual clothes for harbour restaurants. Day-by-day reality check

Day 1: Chaos. Handover, provisioning, finding where the winch handles live. Don't worry. Day 2: First sail. Possibly rough. Possibly perfect. The anchor drill takes longer than expected. Day 3: You've found your rhythm. The morning is magical. The anchor holds. Someone catches a fish. Day 4–6: This is why people sail. New anchorage every night, swimming before breakfast, sundowners in the cockpit. Day 7: Nobody wants to leave.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need sailing experience for a flotilla?
No — flotilla holidays are specifically designed for beginners. Basic boat-handling skills are helpful but the lead boat provides instruction and support throughout.
What if I get seasick every day?
Most people who experience seasickness on day 1 are fine by day 3 as the body adapts. If you're prone to motion sickness generally, use scopolamine patches (prescription) applied the night before departure. Staying on deck, avoiding alcohol, and eating lightly all help.
How do I choose a reputable charter company?
Look for MYBA membership (Mediterranean Yacht Brokers Association) or equivalent. Read independent reviews on Sailing Forums, YachtCharterFleet, and TripAdvisor. Ask specifically about fleet age and maintenance standards. A reputable company will answer these questions directly.
What happens if the weather is too rough to sail?
Your flotilla lead crew or, for bareboat, your own judgement determines whether conditions are safe to sail. Charter companies have weather cancellation policies. Being stuck in a beautiful harbour in bad weather is genuinely enjoyable — treat it as a rest day, not a disaster.
