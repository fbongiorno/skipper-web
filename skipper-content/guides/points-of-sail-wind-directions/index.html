Sailing Wind Directions Explained: Points of Sail Guide

Understanding the relationship between your boat and the wind is the foundation of sailing. This guide explains every point of sail — what they feel like and when to use each.

The no-go zone

A sailboat cannot sail directly into the wind. The no-go zone (also called the "iron zone" or "in irons") is the arc roughly 45° either side of the true wind direction where sails cannot generate drive. If you sail into this zone, your sails flog uselessly and the boat slows to a stop. The solution is to tack — turn through the wind to reach the desired course from the other side.

Close-hauled

Sailing as close to the wind as possible without entering the no-go zone — typically 40–45° off the true wind. Sails are sheeted in tight. This is the point of sail when beating to windward; it's often the hardest work but the most technically rewarding. The boat heels, spray flies, and speed is good but not maximum.

Close reach

Approximately 60–80° off the wind. Sails are eased slightly from close-hauled. One of the most pleasant points of sail — good speed, moderate heel, and the boat feels balanced.

Beam reach

Wind at 90° to the boat — blowing directly across the beam. This is typically the fastest point of sail for most monohulls. Sails are roughly halfway out. The boat rides level and the motion is comfortable.

Broad reach

Wind coming from behind and to one side, typically 120–150° off the bow. Sails are eased well out. Fast and comfortable — the classic trade-wind point of sail for long passages.

Running downwind

Wind directly behind — 180° off the bow. Sails are squared off to either side, or a spinnaker is deployed. Running requires attention to accidental gybes — if the wind shifts across the stern, the boom can slam across with dangerous force. Use a preventer to secure the boom.

Frequently asked questions

What is the fastest point of sail?
For most monohulls, the beam reach (wind at 90°) is fastest. Modern racing yachts and multihulls can be fastest on a broad reach due to apparent wind effects.
Why can't a sailboat sail directly into the wind?
Sails generate lift by creating differential pressure on their two sides. Pointing directly into wind, air hits both sides equally — no pressure differential means no drive.
What does 'tacking' mean?
Tacking is turning the bow of the boat through the wind, switching from one tack (where the wind comes from one side) to the other. It's how you make progress directly upwind in a zig-zag pattern.
