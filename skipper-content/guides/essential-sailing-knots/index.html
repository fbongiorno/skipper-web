Sailing Knots Every Sailor Must Know (With Diagrams)

Ten knots, learned properly, will cover 95% of everything you'll do on a cruising yacht. Here are the ones that matter — and how to tie them correctly.

The bowline

The bowline (boh-lin) is the most important knot in sailing — a fixed loop that won't slip or jam, used for attaching sheets to sails, tying fenders, or creating a temporary mooring loop. To tie it: make a small loop in the standing part, pass the working end up through the loop, around the back of the standing part, and back down through the loop. Remember: "the rabbit comes up through the hole, runs around the tree, and goes back down the hole."

Cleat hitch

Used to secure a line to a cleat on the dock or boat. Take a full turn around the base of the cleat, then make figure-of-eight turns over the horns, finishing with a locking hitch by twisting the last turn before slipping it over the horn.

Round turn & two half hitches

Used to secure a line to a ring, rail, or post — especially useful for attaching fenders. Take a complete round turn around the object, then add two half hitches on the standing part.

Reef knot

Used for tying reef points in a sail. Right over left, left over right. Note: the reef knot is not a general-purpose joining knot — it can capsize under uneven loads. Use a sheet bend for joining two lines of different sizes.

Figure-of-eight stopper knot

Tied at the end of a sheet or halyard to prevent it from running through a block. Essential — without it, an unattended line can disappear aloft at the worst moment.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most important knot to learn first?
The bowline. It creates a secure, fixed loop and has dozens of applications aboard. Once you can tie it confidently with your eyes closed, learn the cleat hitch next.
How do I remember how to tie a bowline?
Use the rabbit analogy: the rabbit (working end) comes up through the hole (small loop), runs around the tree (standing part), and goes back down the hole. It sounds silly but it works.
Is the reef knot the same as a square knot?
Yes — the reef knot is also called a square knot in North America. Right over left, then left over right. It's symmetrical when tied correctly.
