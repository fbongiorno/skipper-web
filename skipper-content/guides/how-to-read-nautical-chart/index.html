How to Read a Nautical Chart: Beginner's Guide

Nautical charts are the sailor's map — and reading them is one of the most important skills you can develop. This guide explains everything from depth lines to buoyage, in plain language.

What is a nautical chart?

A nautical chart is a specialized map of a body of water and its adjacent coastline. Unlike a land map, its primary concern is navigational safety — showing water depth, underwater hazards, buoys, lights, shipping lanes, and anchorages. Charts are produced by national hydrographic offices (the UKHO produces Admiralty charts; NOAA produces US charts) and are updated regularly to reflect changes in buoyage, new wrecks, and shifting channels.

Depth and soundings

Depth is shown on charts as soundings — small numbers scattered across the water indicating the depth at that point. The reference level (called Chart Datum) is the lowest astronomical tide — meaning actual depths are always equal to or greater than the charted sounding. Contour lines connect points of equal depth, just like elevation contours on a land map. The 5-metre and 10-metre contours are particularly important for coastal navigation.

Chart symbols

Charts use standardized symbols to indicate features. The most important for cruising sailors include: anchor symbol (recommended anchorage), wreck symbols (dangerous or non-dangerous), rock symbols (above or below water), light characteristics (describing how a lighthouse or buoy flashes), and restricted areas (military zones, marine reserves). The complete list is published in UKHO publication Symbols and Abbreviations used on Admiralty Charts (NP5011).

Buoys and markers

The IALA buoyage system marks safe water channels and hazards using colored buoys. In IALA Region A (Europe, Africa, Asia, Australia), red buoys mark the port (left) side of a channel when entering from seaward; green buoys mark the starboard side. In IALA Region B (Americas, Japan, Philippines, Korea), these are reversed. Cardinal marks — yellow and black buoys arranged in quadrants — indicate which side of a hazard to pass on.

Plotting a course

Basic course plotting involves three steps: identify your position (using GPS or visual bearings), identify your destination, and draw a straight line between them. Then check that line for hazards — depth contours, rocks, restricted areas — and adjust accordingly. Measure the distance using the latitude scale (1 minute of latitude = 1 nautical mile) and calculate your estimated time of arrival based on boat speed. Don't forget to account for tidal streams and leeway.

Electronic charts (ECS/ECDIS)

Most modern sailors use electronic chart plotters alongside (not instead of) paper charts. Chart plotters integrate GPS position with a digital chart database, making navigation significantly easier. The most popular consumer chart systems are Navionics and C-MAP. Always carry up-to-date paper charts as backup — electronics fail.

Frequently asked questions

What chart scale should I use for coastal sailing?
Large-scale charts (1:10,000 to 1:50,000) give the detail needed for harbours and coastal navigation. Small-scale charts (1:100,000 and smaller) are better for passage planning.
Are electronic charts as accurate as paper charts?
Quality electronic charts from Navionics or C-MAP are highly accurate, but they're only as good as their underlying survey data. Always cross-reference with the latest paper chart, especially in poorly-surveyed areas.
What is Chart Datum?
Chart Datum is the reference level from which chart depths are measured. It's typically set at the Lowest Astronomical Tide (LAT) — the lowest tide that can be predicted under average meteorological conditions.
