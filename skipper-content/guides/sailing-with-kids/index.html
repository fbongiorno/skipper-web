Sailing with Kids: The Complete Family Charter Guide

Sailing with children is one of the most rewarding family travel experiences — and one of the most intimidating to plan. This guide covers everything from the best destinations to keeping a 7-year-old entertained on a 5-hour passage.

Is sailing safe for children?

Sailing with children is safe when properly planned — in fact, children often take to life afloat better than adults. The ocean is a genuinely enriching environment: kids develop spatial awareness, swimming confidence, teamwork, and a connection with nature that's hard to replicate on a beach holiday. The key is choosing the right destination, the right boat, and the right safety equipment.

What age can children sail?

Babies and toddlers (0–3) can sail on charter yachts. The main considerations are sun protection (infants burn fast on the water), sleep routine management, and dinghy safety. A catamaran with its stable, flat deck is strongly recommended for babies. Most experienced sailing families say 3–5 is the ideal starting age — children are old enough to understand basic rules, enjoy the sea, and participate in simple tasks.

Children aged 6–12 are the ideal sailing age. Old enough to help with ropes and winches, young enough to find anchoring in a new bay genuinely magical. Teenagers who have grown up sailing are often the most capable crew on the boat.

Best destinations for families

The best family sailing destinations share four characteristics: short passages, sheltered anchorages, warm clear water for swimming, and good provisioning. Our top picks:

  • British Virgin Islands — the world's best family sailing destination. Passages under 10nm, flat water in the Sir Francis Drake Channel, excellent provisioning, and the Baths (children love the boulder pools). Warm year-round.
  • Ionian Islands, Greece — lighter winds than the Aegean, calm anchorages, warm water from June, and the sheer beauty of Kefalonia and Ithaka. Lefkada is the best base for families.
  • The Grenadines — older children (10+) thrive here. The Tobago Cays snorkeling is extraordinary; Bequia's Admiralty Bay is a safe anchorage with a dinghy dock. Passages are longer than the BVI.
  • Croatia — ideal for families with older children. Calm Adriatic waters, medieval harbour towns, excellent local food, and passages of 20–30nm that keep everyone engaged.

Catamaran vs. monohull for families

For families with young children, a catamaran is strongly recommended. The reasons are practical: a catamaran's wide, flat deck provides far more safe play space than a monohull's narrow side decks. The boat doesn't heel significantly, reducing the risk of children sliding around below decks or on deck. Multiple cabins mean children and adults sleep separately. The large cockpit table works as a meal station, games table, and activity centre.

Monohulls are fine for older children (10+) and for sailing families who want the full sailing experience. For babies, toddlers, and mixed-age groups, a catamaran removes most of the stress. See our catamaran vs. monohull comparison for full details.

Safety essentials

Non-negotiable safety items for sailing with children:

  • Correctly fitted lifejackets — worn at all times when on deck underway, and when on the bow or stern in any conditions. Most charter companies provide children's lifejackets; always verify the fit before departure.
  • Safety harnesses and jackstays — children should be clipped on whenever conditions are rough or at night. A dedicated children's harness that fits properly is essential.
  • Lifelines and netting — many charter companies can rig additional safety netting between the lifelines on request, particularly valuable for toddlers.
  • Dinghy safety — establish strict rules for dinghy use from day one. Children must wear lifejackets in the dinghy at all times, without exception.
  • Sun protection — children burn faster on the water than on a beach. SPF 50+ sunscreen applied every 2 hours, UV-protective swimwear, hats, and shade are essential.

Keeping kids engaged on passage

The biggest challenge of sailing with children is long passages. Strategies that work:

  • Give them jobs — children love responsibility. A 6-year-old can hold the depth sounder, a 10-year-old can read the chart, a teenager can take the helm. Engaged crew are happy crew.
  • Fishing lines — a simple trolling line takes 2 minutes to rig and occupies children for hours.
  • Keep passages short — plan your itinerary around 2–3 hour passages maximum for young children. A 5-hour passage that's fine for adults can be miserable for a 5-year-old.
  • Swimming stops — anchor for lunch in a pretty bay rather than sailing through. The anticipation of a swim keeps children motivated on passage.

Packing for children

In addition to the standard sailing packing list, bring for children: properly fitted lifejackets and harness (not the boat's), UV swimwear (long-sleeved rashguard), reef-safe sunscreen SPF 50+, seasickness patches or Stugeron (consult your doctor for appropriate children's dosage), a waterproof torch for night-time toilet visits, snorkeling equipment (fins and mask fitted for your child), and a kindle loaded with books and entertainment for longer passages.

Frequently asked questions

What age is too young for sailing?
No age is too young with the right preparation. Babies have sailed circumnavigations. The key is a stable boat (catamaran), calm sailing conditions, sun protection, and properly fitted infant lifejackets.
Do charter companies allow babies on board?
Most do, though some have minimum age policies (typically 6 months or 1 year). Always declare ages when booking. Some companies charge a small supplement for infant lifejacket hire.
How do I handle seasickness in children?
Seasickness is common in children under 10. The best prevention is keeping them on deck in fresh air, looking at the horizon, and giving them tasks. For longer passages, scopolamine patches (prescription) or Stugeron (over-counter in Europe) are effective — consult your doctor before the trip.
What if my child falls overboard?
Practice the man-overboard drill with your whole crew including children before departure. Every child old enough to understand should know the MOB procedure: shout 'man overboard', point at the person in the water, and never take your eyes off them. Charter companies will brief you on the procedure.
