What to Expect on a Crewed Yacht Charter: A First-Timer's Guide

Crewed charters are the ultimate sailing holiday — but knowing what to expect makes the difference between a good trip and an extraordinary one. Here's everything first-timers need to know.

What's included

A crewed charter typically includes the yacht, the captain, and (on most vessels of 45ft+) a chef/hostess. The captain handles all navigation and seamanship; the chef handles meals, provisioning, and cabin service. Fuel, food, and marina fees are usually covered by the APA (see below). Alcohol may or may not be included depending on the charter contract — clarify this in advance.

Working with your captain and crew

The captain is legally responsible for the vessel and the safety of everyone aboard — their word on matters of navigation and safety is final. Within those parameters, the best captains are incredibly accommodating: they'll adjust the itinerary to your preferences, suggest hidden anchorages you'd never find on your own, and adapt mealtimes and pace to suit the group. Brief your captain on day one: dietary requirements, activity preferences, how early you like to get underway, and any special occasions to celebrate.

The APA system

The Advance Provisioning Allowance (APA) is a fund — typically 25–35% of the base charter rate — deposited with the captain at the start of the trip. It covers fuel, provisioning, marina fees, and any other running costs. The captain keeps receipts for all expenditure; a full accounting is provided at the end of the trip, with any unused APA returned or additional costs invoiced. It's a transparent system once you understand it.

Tipping etiquette

Tips are a significant part of crew income in the charter industry. Industry standard is 10–15% of the base charter rate for excellent service, given in cash (euros or dollars) to the captain at the end of the trip. They distribute it to the crew. Don't feel obligated to tip for average service — the 10–15% guideline is for genuinely outstanding work.

Getting the most from your charter

Brief your captain thoroughly on day one. Share your itinerary preferences but stay open to the captain's local knowledge — the best anchorages are rarely in the guidebook. Bring a wishlist of dishes or wines to brief the chef. Communicate openly about the pace: some groups want to sail hard; others prefer to anchor early and swim all afternoon. Neither is wrong.

Frequently asked questions

Can we participate in sailing the boat on a crewed charter?
Absolutely — most captains welcome guests who want to take the helm, handle lines, or learn about navigation. Just ask.
What happens if we want to change the itinerary mid-trip?
Crewed charters are flexible by nature. Your captain will advise on feasibility (weather, distance) and adapt the plan where possible. Clarity on day one reduces mid-trip friction.
Is food good on crewed charters?
On reputable crewed charters, the food is genuinely excellent — often restaurant quality. Brief the chef on dietary requirements and preferences before the trip, not after departure.
