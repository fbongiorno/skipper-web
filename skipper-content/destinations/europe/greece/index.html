Sailing in Greece: Complete Guide to Greek Island Sailing (2026)

Greece is the world's most celebrated sailing ground — 6,000 islands, 16,000km of coastline, and the most reliable summer winds in the Mediterranean. This guide covers everything you need to plan a Greek island sailing trip.

Overview

Greece consistently tops polls of the world's best sailing destinations, and for good reason. The country's extraordinary geography — a scattered archipelago across the Aegean and Ionian seas — creates an unparalleled playground for sailors of every experience level. Whether you're island-hopping through the Cyclades on a 7-day bareboat charter or cruising the Ionian on a crewed yacht, you'll find sheltered anchorages, deep clear water, and some of the finest tavernas in Europe just a short dinghy ride from your anchor.

Greece's reliable summer winds are its defining feature. The meltemi — a northerly thermal wind that builds in the Aegean each afternoon through July and August — provides exhilarating downwind sailing and keeps the temperatures bearable. For those who prefer lighter air, the Ionian coast to the west offers gentler conditions and equally spectacular scenery.

Best sailing areas

Greece divides naturally into two distinct sailing regions, each with its own character and appeal.

The Cyclades

The iconic whitewashed villages of Mykonos, the caldera of Santorini, the traditional harbor towns of Paros and Naxos — the Cyclades are the postcard image of Greek sailing. This is the Aegean at its most dramatic. Expect strong meltemi winds in July and August (force 4–6 regularly, sometimes more), excellent sailing conditions for confident intermediate and advanced sailors, and some of the most photogenic anchorages in the world.

Popular routes start from Athens (Lavrion or Alimos marinas) and loop through Kea, Syros, Mykonos, Naxos, and Paros before returning. The 7-day Cyclades circuit is one of the world's great sailing itineraries.

The Ionian Islands

Corfu, Lefkada, Kefalonia, Ithaka, Zakynthos — the Ionian chain runs down Greece's western coast and offers a gentler sailing experience. Winds are lighter and more predictable than the Aegean, making the Ionian the ideal destination for beginner sailors and families. The scenery is lush and green rather than stark and white, with a distinct Italian influence from centuries of Venetian rule. Lefkada is the main charter base, perfectly positioned for a 7- or 10-day island circuit.

Best time to go

The Greek sailing season runs from April through October, with peak season in July and August. May, June, and September offer the best balance of good weather, warm water, and fewer crowds. The meltemi is absent in spring and autumn, making these months better for less experienced sailors.

Charter bases & marinas

The main charter bases are Athens (Lavrion/Alimos) for the Cyclades and Saronic Gulf, and Lefkada for the Ionian. Corfu and Kos also have established charter fleets. Greece has excellent marina infrastructure, with well-equipped facilities in most major ports and dozens of smaller municipal quays throughout the islands.

Costs & budgeting

A bareboat catamaran in high season (July–August) typically costs €3,500–€7,000 per week depending on size and specification. Monohulls range from €1,200 to €3,500. Add provisioning (€100–€150/day for 6 people), marina fees (€20–€80/night), and fuel for a complete budget. See our full sailing trip budget guide for a detailed breakdown.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a sailing license to charter in Greece?
Greece accepts a wide range of qualifications including RYA Day Skipper, ASA 104, and ICC. Some charter companies also accept a declaration of competence for smaller vessels. See our full guide to charter licenses for details.
What is the meltemi wind?
The meltemi is a dry northerly wind that blows across the Aegean in summer, typically force 3–5 but occasionally reaching force 7–8. It builds in the afternoon and eases at night. It's what makes Aegean sailing so exhilarating — and occasionally challenging.
Which is better for beginners — the Ionian or Aegean?
The Ionian Islands are strongly recommended for beginner sailors. Winds are lighter (typically force 2–3), seas are calmer, and the anchorages are more sheltered. The Aegean and Cyclades are better suited to intermediate and advanced sailors.
How far in advance should I book a Greek island charter?
For July and August, book at least 6 months in advance — the best boats go quickly. May, June, and September have more availability and often better prices.
