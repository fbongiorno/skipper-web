Sailing in Turkey: Aegean & Mediterranean Sailing Guide

Turkey's so-called Turquoise Coast — from Bodrum to Antalya — combines Aegean sailing with ancient ruins, exceptional food, and some of the most secluded anchorages in the Mediterranean. And it costs significantly less than Greece.

Overview

Turkey's Aegean and Mediterranean coasts offer an exceptional sailing experience that combines Greek-quality scenery with lower prices, richer history, and arguably even better food. The region known as the Turquoise Coast or Mavi Yolculuk (Blue Voyage) stretches from Bodrum in the north to Antalya in the east, encompassing the gulfs of Gökova, Hisarönü, Bozburun, and Fethiye.

Best sailing areas

The Gulf of Gökova, south of Bodrum, is one of the most beautiful sailing areas in the world — a long, sheltered gulf lined with forested mountains and dotted with tiny islets and hidden bays. The Bozburun Peninsula offers some of the most secluded anchorages on the Turkish coast, popular with gulet charters for exactly that reason. Fethiye Bay and its famous Twelve Islands circuit is an essential highlight, combining gorgeous anchorages with the nearby ghost town of Kayaköy and the travertine terraces of Pamukkale (inland excursion).

Best anchorages

Cleopatra's Island (Sedir Adası) — famous for its Roman-era sand, supposedly imported from Egypt, with a protected bay ideal for swimming. English Harbour, Göcek — a sheltered marina town surrounded by islands, perfect as a base. Butterfly Valley — accessible only by sea, a hidden canyon home to rare butterflies and a small camp. Ölüdeniz lagoon — one of the most photographed bays in the Mediterranean; anchor outside and dinghy in.

Gulet charters

Turkey is the home of the gulet — a traditional wooden motorsailer that forms the backbone of Turkish charter culture. Crewed gulet charters are excellent value compared to crewed yachts elsewhere in the Med: a 10-cabin gulet for 20 guests might cost €15,000–€25,000 per week all-inclusive, including captain, crew, and all meals. Perfect for groups, families, or corporate charters.

Best time to go

May and October are ideal — excellent weather, lower prices, and significantly fewer boats. July and August are peak season: warm, busy, and occasionally windy (the imbat afternoon sea breeze freshens in summer). The Turkish coast enjoys one of the Mediterranean's longest seasons, with comfortable sailing possible from April through November.

Frequently asked questions

Is Turkey cheaper than Greece for sailing?
Significantly so. Marina fees, provisioning, and charter rates are all 20–40% lower than equivalent Greek destinations, making Turkey excellent value.
Can I sail between Turkey and Greece?
Yes, with the right clearance. Day trips to Greek islands near the Turkish coast (Rhodes, Kos, Samos) are popular, but you must clear customs at both ends.
What is a gulet charter?
A gulet is a traditional Turkish wooden motorsailer, typically crewed, used for Blue Voyage holidays along the Turkish coast. They range from small 4-cabin vessels to large 12-cabin ships.
