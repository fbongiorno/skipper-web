Caribbean Sailing Guide: Best Islands & Routes for 2026

Trade winds. Rum punches. Pristine reefs. The Caribbean is sailing paradise — and with dozens of island chains offering completely different characters, this guide helps you choose the right one.

Overview

The Caribbean is defined by its northeast trade winds — steady, reliable, and warm — that blow 15–25 knots across the Atlantic and push sailors effortlessly from island to island. The challenge isn't finding wind; it's choosing which islands to visit. The Caribbean arc stretches over 4,000km from the Bahamas in the north to Trinidad in the south, encompassing hundreds of islands with radically different characters.

British Virgin Islands

The BVI are the world's most popular bareboat charter destination — for good reason. Short passages, excellent infrastructure, and the sheltered Sir Francis Drake Channel make this the ideal first charter destination. The downside: it's also the busiest. Popular anchorages like The Bight and Cane Garden Bay are crowded in peak season.

The Grenadines

For many experienced sailors, the Grenadines represent the Caribbean at its finest. The chain of small islands running south from St. Vincent to Grenada combines spectacular scenery — the volcanic peaks of the Windwards, the palm-fringed beaches of the Tobago Cays — with a distinctly less touristy atmosphere than the BVI. Passages are longer and trade winds more challenging, making this better suited to intermediate and experienced sailors.

Windward Islands

Martinique, St. Lucia, St. Vincent, and Grenada form the Windward Islands — French-influenced, lushly volcanic, and far less charter-saturated than the BVI. St. Lucia's Rodney Bay is a major charter base, perfectly positioned for a circuit of the southern Windwards. Martinique adds Creole cuisine and French wine to the mix. Grenada, the "Spice Isle," is the turnaround point for many Windwards itineraries.

Best time to go

The Caribbean sailing season runs mid-November through late April. December through February offers the most reliable trade winds. March and April are excellent but increasingly busy as northern hemisphere sailors escape winter. May through November is hurricane season — most charter operators reduce fleets significantly and insurance restrictions apply.

Frequently asked questions

Which Caribbean island is best for beginners?
The BVI is widely recommended for first-time charter sailors. The passages are short, the winds are steady (not overpowering), and the infrastructure is excellent.
Do I need to clear customs between Caribbean islands?
Yes, most islands require clearing customs on arrival and departure, even between nearby territories. The BVI is an exception — no customs between its own islands.
Is the Caribbean expensive for sailing holidays?
Charter rates are higher than the Mediterranean, but the experience is different. Provisioning and marina fees are comparable; flights from Europe are the main added cost.
