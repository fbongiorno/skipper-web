Sailing in the British Virgin Islands: BVI Charter Guide

The British Virgin Islands are the world's bareboat charter capital — steady trade winds, flat water, short passages, and no customs between islands. If you're planning your first charter, start here.

Why the BVI is the world's charter capital

The British Virgin Islands earned their reputation through geography and luck. The 60-odd islands and cays are perfectly spaced for day-sailing — most passages are under 10 miles, sheltered by the island chain from Atlantic swells, and blessed with consistent 15–20 knot trade winds from the east. You can anchor in a new bay every night, snorkel on a pristine reef, dine on floating restaurants, and never once worry about a night passage.

The Sir Francis Drake Channel — the sheltered waterway between the main island chain and the outer islands — is the highway of BVI sailing. It's one of the most pleasurable day-sailing venues on earth.

Best anchorages

The Bight, Norman Island — the island said to have inspired Treasure Island, with excellent snorkeling at the caves on the western point. The Baths, Virgin Gorda — extraordinary granite boulders creating natural pools and grottoes; get there early before the day-trippers arrive. Sandy Cay — an uninhabited island with a perfect crescent beach, ideal for a lunchtime stop. Soper's Hole, Tortola — a well-protected western anchorage with good provisioning and a lively waterfront.

Must-visit spots

No BVI trip is complete without a stop at the Soggy Dollar Bar on Jost Van Dyke — rumored birthplace of the Painkiller cocktail, accessible only by dinghy. Anegada, the BVI's coral atoll and flat outlier, offers world-class bonefishing and the freshest lobster in the Caribbean. Allow an extra day if you venture there.

Best time to go

December through April is peak season — trade winds are reliable, skies are clear, and temperatures are perfect (27–29°C). Christmas week and Easter are the busiest and most expensive times. May and November are shoulder season with fewer crowds and slightly variable winds. Avoid June through October (hurricane season).

Charter information

Tortola's Road Town is home to the Caribbean's largest charter fleets, including The Moorings, Sunsail, and Leopard Catamarans. A bareboat charter for 6–8 people typically runs $4,000–$8,000/week for a monohull and $7,000–$15,000 for a catamaran in peak season. Add provisioning, fuel, mooring fees ($30–$40/night at most popular spots), and the BVI cruising permit ($75 per trip).

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a licence to charter in the BVI?
The BVI requires proof of sailing experience rather than a formal licence. Most charter companies accept a resume of experience; a coastal/offshore sailing certificate from RYA or ASA adds confidence.
Can I sail to the US Virgin Islands from the BVI?
Yes, but you must clear customs at both ends. The USVI's St. John is a popular day trip from the BVI. Ensure your charter agreement allows international travel.
How many BVI islands can I visit in a week?
A typical 7-day charter covers 8–12 stops comfortably, given the short passages. Don't rush — the BVI rewards those who linger in a good anchorage.
