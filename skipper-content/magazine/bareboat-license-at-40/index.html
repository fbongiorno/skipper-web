From Zero to Skipper: How I Got My Bareboat License at 40

One sailor's journey from nervous beginner to confident bareboat skipper. Courses, costs, time commitment, and whether it's really worth it.

Where it started

I was 40 years old, had never set foot on a sailing yacht, and had just booked a bareboat charter in Croatia for the following summer. This is, in retrospect, exactly backwards from how you're supposed to do it. You're supposed to learn to sail, get qualified, log some miles, and then book the charter. I booked the charter and then panicked into learning to sail.

Eighteen months later, I was anchored in a perfect bay in the Ionian Islands, my wife and two children asleep below decks, watching a full moon rise over a Greek island. It was worth every pound and every moment of terror.

The course

I took the RYA Day Skipper theory course first — an online course that took about three months of evenings to complete. It covered meteorology, celestial navigation theory (largely irrelevant but interesting), pilotage, collision regulations, and passage planning. Then the practical week: five days aboard a 38ft Beneteau with four other students and an instructor, sailing from Plymouth. I was sick the first morning. By the third day, I was genuinely absorbed.

Was it worth it?

The short answer: yes, unequivocally. The slightly longer answer: learning to sail at 40 is no harder than learning at 25, the qualification opens up a type of travel that no other mode of transport can offer, and the sense of competence that comes from navigating your family safely into an anchorage you've never visited, in a country you don't speak the language of, is something that a hotel holiday simply cannot replicate.

Frequently asked questions

Is it hard to get an RYA Day Skipper at 40?
Not particularly. Sailing is a practical skill that improves with time on the water. The theory requires study; the practical is accessible to most people of average fitness.
How much does an RYA Day Skipper course cost?
The practical 5-day course costs approximately £900–£1,200 in the UK. Add the theory course (£150–£250 online) and ICC application fee (£55 from RYA) for a total around £1,200–£1,500.
