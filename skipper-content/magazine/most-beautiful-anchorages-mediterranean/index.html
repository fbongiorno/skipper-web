The 10 Most Beautiful Anchorages in the Mediterranean

From hidden Adriatic coves to Aegean sea caves — the ten most breathtaking anchorages in the Mediterranean, chosen by experienced sailors and charter skippers.

Overview

Ask ten experienced Mediterranean sailors for their favourite anchorage and you'll get ten different answers. The Mediterranean's 46,000km of coastline contains thousands of places to drop anchor — from crowded tourist bays to deserted coves only accessible by boat. These are the ten that come up again and again in the conversations of those who know these waters best.

The anchorages

1. Cala Luna, Sardinia — A spectacular limestone gorge on Sardinia's east coast, accessible only by boat or a 3-hour hike. The turquoise water against white rock is otherworldly. 2. Kleopatra's Island (Sedir Adası), Turkey — The bay is famous for its Roman-era sand; the anchorage, looking up at pine-forested hills, is one of the most peaceful in the Aegean. 3. Kotor Bay, Montenegro — Not strictly the Mediterranean, but the Adriatic's finest enclosed anchorage: medieval walled city, dramatic mountains, perfectly sheltered water. 4. Amorgos, Cyclades — The eastern Cyclades' most dramatic island, famous for the film 'The Big Blue'. Anchor in Aegiali bay and take the moped road to the clifftop monastery. 5. Vis, Croatia — The most remote inhabited Croatian island, with the clearest water in the Adriatic. 6. Kalkan, Turkey — A perfectly preserved Ottoman harbour town in a sheltered bay on the Turkish Aegean. 7. Calanque de Morgiou, France — The finest of Marseille's calanques: sheer white limestone walls, emerald water, a tiny fishing village at the head. 8. Stari Grad, Hvar — The quieter, more authentic side of Hvar: a long sheltered bay, a Renaissance town, and UNESCO World Heritage farmland behind. 9. Paxos, Ionian — Small, perfect, and covered in ancient olive groves. The sea caves at the northern tip (Blue Grotto) rival Capri. 10. Ölüdeniz, Turkey — The Blue Lagoon: a protected national park lagoon accessible only from the sea. Anchor outside; dinghy in through the narrow entrance.

Frequently asked questions

How do I find lesser-known anchorages?
Local knowledge is irreplaceable. Brief your charter company; buy the dedicated pilot book for your sailing area; and talk to other sailors in harbour — the cruising community shares information generously.
Do I need special permission to anchor in marine reserves?
Yes — many national parks and marine reserves have restricted anchorage zones or permit requirements. Check the current regulations for your destination before arrival. In Greece, several protected anchorages require a daily permit.
